Kata: Converting Strings to JadenCase

Description of the Problem

Jaden Smith, the son of actor Will Smith, is widely recognized not only for his film roles in movies like The Karate Kid (2010) and After Earth (2013), but also for his unusual style of writing on social media. One of the most distinctive traits in his Tweets is that he capitalizes Every Single Word, regardless of grammatical rules.

The goal of this kata is to transform any given string into the same style. This format is commonly referred to as JadenCase. Every word must begin with an uppercase letter while the rest remains lowercase. Contractions such as aren't must remain intact while still having the first letter capitalized (Aren't).

Example:

Not Jaden-Cased: "How can mirrors be real if our eyes aren't real"
Jaden-Cased:     "How Can Mirrors Be Real If Our Eyes Aren't Real"

My Solution

def to_jaden_case(string):
    return " ".join([word.capitalize() for word in string.split()])

Best Solution

def to_jaden_case(string):
    return string.title()

Detailed Explanation and Comparison

Both solutions correctly convert sentences into JadenCase, but they differ in how they handle capitalization and how they operate under the hood. Your solution uses a more explicit and deliberate approach: it splits the string into words manually, capitalizes each word individually using capitalize(), and then reconstructs the sentence with join(). This is a clean and very readable method because the transformation is performed step by step.

The best solution takes advantage of Python's built-in title() method, which was designed to convert strings so that every word begins with an uppercase letter and the remainder becomes lowercase automatically. This produces the same result in a shorter and more concise form.

However, it’s worth noting a subtle detail: title() can behave differently with special cases, such as words containing apostrophes. For standard English contractions like “aren’t,” both solutions behave identically, but in more complex strings, title() may split words in ways you do not expect. Despite that, for this kata and ordinary sentences, the title() implementation is perfectly suitable and highly efficient.

Overall, your solution is explicit, transparent, and instructional, while the best solution is minimalistic, built-in, and optimized. Both pass the kata, but title() is often preferred for its simplicity and speed.
